Stacks

Supporting listening goals for music conoisseurs to organize music they intend to listen to.

Skills

Research, interaction design, user testing

Tools

Figma

Timeline

8 weeks

I enjoy finding new music to listen to. For instance, when an artist releases a new album or a friend recommends an interesting track.

However, I’m not always in the mood to listen to something new. There are days I’d rather have Taylor Swift on repeat than listen to the new Black Country, New Road with 4298 experimental sounds.

So now I have to record the name of the album and its artist with the intention of listening to it later.

Who is our primary user?

Not everyone listens to music the same way. Our primary user can be labelled as a music conoisseur.

These are users who actively search for new music to listen. User research revealed their goals are to listen to a lot of music from a variety of artists or genres.

Problem

Current methods used to keep track of music the user intends to listen to are not flexible.

This leads to disorganization. Music is not discoverable so it is difficult to decide what to listen to. As a result, earlier entries are forgotten, and less music is actually listened to.

Solution

Save music to listen to later

Save new and interesting music into a 'Listen later' list. Organize music into categorized folders with other new finds.

Rediscover new music that was previously saved

Search and filter from music saved into ‘Listen later’ to rediscover music that fits the current mood and interested genres.

Track listening progress on collections and albums

Motivate users to listen to more music with a visual representation of listening progress.

Project goals

i.e. Why did you do this project, Jennifer?

The goals of this project were largely for personal growth. At this point, I had designed at several B2B startups. I was often isolated from users and relied on designing with assumptions.

The primary goals of this project were to test my assumptions by speaking to users when it made sense and incorporating feedback into my design process to inform my interaction design decisions.

Outcome

Throughout the project, I spoke to users at three stages of the design process: facilitating user research, concept testing with low-fidelity wireframes, and usability testing after medium-fidelity prototypes.

Overall, there were three iterations of the project. Interactive design decisions made on successive iterations used feedback from previous testing.

Learnings

Refinement is a iterative process

When I was designing my second iteration of prototypes, I had no idea that there would be more iterations. I thought I was moving into high-fidelity prototyping but in the end, this iteration was definitely more medium fidelity. I learned to trust the process and really felt that user testing was what challenged my craft and interaction design skills. Without it, I would have stopped after my second iteration with an end product that was less refined.

Next steps

Consider a broader audience of users

I’d like to consider how a broader audience of users would use this application. This concept is very niche to a narrow audience of users who listen to music in a certain way. I’d like to explore how other types of listeners could benefit from a product feature like this.

Discovery

Validating the problem and finding pain points

I talked to users and searched internet forums to first validate that this problem was not my own and additionally understand the associated pain points.

Design goals

1. Provide a flexible system for organizing music the user intends to listen to later.
2. Improve discoverability of music.
3. Help users decide what music to listen to.

Ideation

I used user stories to ideate on each of the design goals.

The first iteration

Wireframes

What did I learn after concept testing these wireframes?
Insights

  1. Users often use album covers to decide what to listen to

  2. What a user wants to listen to is largely dependent on their mood

  3. Users want to organize music into categories but the categories themselves vary all the time (can be by genre, mood, artist, etc.)

Recommendations

  1. Increase the card size of albums

  2. Search & filter by mood

  3. Create a flexible organization system by letting users categorize their own music

The second iteration

Prototypes

What did I learn after user testing these prototypes?
Insights

  1. Progress tracking is confusing — language is difficult to interpret

  2. Users are unsure what to expect when tracks are shuffled — what order would they play in?

Recommendations

  1. Redesign progress tracking with more explicit language

  2. Provide visual feedback to user when they tap ‘Shuffle play’

The third iteration

Refinement & interaction design

Refining progress tracking

Tabs were used with explicit in progress, not started, and listened labels.

Refining shuffle play

A queue was added to provide visual feedback and let the user know what order tracks would be played when ‘Shuffle play’ was tapped.

Refining categories

When users make categories, it is important to be able to add both albums and individual tracks. Flexible organization systems for users would accomodate both.

Albums and tracks differed in appearances, which created some visual imbalance. A toggle was added so users can list albums as a group of tracks or track-by-track, which helped with visual imbalance.

The language for “category” also needed some refining. A few options were explored before I finally decided on “stack”.
