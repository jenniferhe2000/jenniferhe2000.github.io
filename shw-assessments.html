Tutor Assessment Tool

Designing an assessment tool MVP for peer tutors

Team

Me (Lead Product Designer)

Akshit D (PM & Engineer)

Akshay R (PM & Engineer)

Jackie L (Product Designer)

Duration

Jun - Jul 2022

Background

About Schoolhouse.world

Schoolhouse.world is an education initiative started by Sal Khan (creator of Khan Academy) to help provide quality, free online tutoring to anyone and everyone who wants to learn. The beating heart of Schoolhouse is its community of volunteer tutors. The community comprises 2320+ highschool & undergraduate tutors and 15500+ learners around the world.

Brief

It is generally agreed upon that assessments are disliked, but unfortunately they’re also the standard to evaluating learners. Our community of peer tutors was asking for an in-platform assessment tool – a way for them to create assessments for their learners directly within the Schoolhouse platform.

Scope

Prioritize features to design an assessment tool MVP that creates value for peer tutors.

Business goal

Schoolhouse was hosting SAT prep sessions in the fall and needed an in-platform assessment tool to build SAT practice tests. These are given to learners to complete and practice throughout each session.

Create SAT practice assessments for learners who attend SAT prep sessions.

Target User

The target user is a peer tutor on Schoolhouse.

User goals

We conducted interviews with 8 tutors on the Schoolhouse platform to uncover user goals when using assessments in sessions.

Create assessments with multiple choice and short answer questions.

Support the course material covered in tutoring sessions & series.

Assess whether learners understood the learning material presented in tutoring sessions & series.

Provide feedback to learners to guide learning.

Pain points

Participation in assessments is low.

Assessment materials, results, and feedback are not centralized.

It was revealed that sessions where tutors assigned assessments to learners had low participation, which is discouraging to tutors who spend time creating the assessment material. Additionally, assessment materials, results, and feedback were not centralized between Schoolhouse and the third-party platforms tutors currently used to create assessments.

Feature prioritization

In-platform assessments would be a new feature on Schoolhouse so there needed to be prioritization for the MVP. As a team, we prioritized high impact and low effort features.

High impact features were those which immediately brought value to user goals. For example, the ability to create assessments with different question types.

Low effort features were those which were feasible for engineers in the given time frame (about 3 months).

Product requirements

  1. Tutors to create assessments.

  2. Tutors to manage assessments they create.

  3. Tutors to assign assessments to learners.

  4. Tutors to view graded assessments and feedback.

  5. Learners to view graded assessments and feedback.

  6. Learners to access and take assessments.

1 – Tutors to create assessments

Design Decision 1

Adding an answer key is optional.

Why did we make this decision?

  • Tutors agree that assessments are stressful for learners.

  • Tutors may choose to create ungraded assessments which are more engaging for learners.

  • Tutors may also create surveys or polls which are fundamentally ungraded.

Other explorations

  • This option was not intuitive.

  • There is no indication that selecting the checkbox marks it as the correct answer.

  • Works well for multiple choice questions but is not reusable with other question types.

Design Decision 2

Sidebar for scrolling to questions.

Why did we make this decision?

  • SAT practice assessments will have 150+ questions.

  • Enables the user to navigate between questions.

Other explorations
Why did we explore this?

  • This was an existing design pattern.

  • Users would not be able to edit an assessment once it is published.

  • With this design, users were required to review their assessment before publishing.

Why didn’t we end up doing this?

  • Was a solution to a problem that was discovered after engineers began development.

  • A compromise was made to add a warning before publishing.

2 – Tutors to manage assessments they create

Design Decision 3

Created assessments are listed in a table.

Why did we make this decision?

A table works best when the tutor wants high-level visibility of the assessments they create.

Other explorations

Cards are not ideal for providing high-level visibility because information is not scannable.

3 – Tutors to assign assessments to learners

Design Decision 4

Tutors assign assessments from a series page.

Why did we make this decision?

  • A series is a set of at least 2 tutoring sessions.

  • The series page provides information on all sessions within in the series.

  • Tutors will have context on each session as they assign assessments to them.

Other explorations

Past explorations were dismissed because they required too many steps or had issues with copy.

Design Decision 5

Assessments assigned to sessions are listed in cards.

Why did we make this decision?

  • One concern tutors had was how assessments reduced participation rates.

  • Most assessments created by tutors are low-stakes, ungraded, or surveys.

  • Cards will display descriptions of each assessment, which learners can read to understand the context of each assessment.

Other explorations

Tables were also considered, but do not work well to display descriptions that learners will need to read in order to understand the context of each assessment.

4 – Tutors to view graded assessments and feedback

Design Decision 6

Tutors can view a summary of all responses.

Why did we make this decision?

  • SAT practice assessments will have 150+ questions.

  • Provides a concise summary of all responses.

Design Decision 7

Tutors can view responses by question and by learner.

Why did we make this decision?

  • A Schoolhouse in-platform assessment tool provides value to tutors over third-party form builders like Kahoot and Google forms because all responses will be centralized with course material and feedback.

  • A part of centralization is monitoring the learners’ grades and responses throughout several sessions.

5 – Learners to view graded assessments and feedback

Design Decision 8

Feedback should be a prioritized for learners to see.

Why did we make this decision?

  • Tutors value giving feedback to learners.

  • Feedback is the opportunity for learners to have context on their grades.

Other explorations

A feedback button hides feedback in a modal so it is less accessible to learners.

6 – Learners to access and take assessments

Design Decision 9

Learners access and take assessments from a series page.

Why did we make this decision?

  • Learners will visit a series page to learn what material covered in each session.

  • Learners should have awareness of the context around each assessment they are assigned before they take it.

Other explorations

  • Tutors are worried about low participation when assessments are assigned to a session.

  • Previous explorations made the assessments too prominent, they should not be the most salient feature of a session.

Learnings

Feature prioritization for an MVP

During user research, we found that tutors typically used assessments within an informal or fun context because they did not want to subject their learners to the pressure that comes with taking assessments. However, a required use case for an in-platform assessment tool was the SAT prep sessions which typically had high attendance rates for Schoolhouse. SAT practice assessments are formal and graded, which contrasts what we learned from tutors. There seemed to be two use cases that contradicted each other.

Ultimately, we decided to prioritize the SAT use case because the features required for this use case aligned with what was necessary for an MVP. As a baseline, we needed to ensure that we had the framework for creating assessments, grading assessments, and reviewing responses before we could think about fun elements like gamification.

How to work in parallel with engineers REMOTELY

Due to timelines, development on this feature began before finalizing some of the design decisions. To be honest, if I were to lead the design on this project again, I would halt engineering efforts for an additional week, but I understood that it was a lot of work for one summer.

At first, I felt rushed and pressured to make decisions because I didn’t want to hold back engineering progress or make engineers change something that they already started. Some changes were quick, but some solutions weren’t as obvious and I knew as a designer it was my responsibility to explore the problem space and various design options if that was required. I learned to give them a heads up if we uncovered a potential design problem that would require additional time. Constant design updates in Slack and the project tracker were also important so there was visibility into my progress and updates.

Just because it’s a big new feature doesn’t mean we have to draw attention to it

Because this feature was rather large and new, my bias as a designer was that we had to advertise it across the platform. This meant the feature felt more complex than it had to be. Later on, I checked my biases and adjusted the scope of the feature. Especially since it was generally agreed upon by tutors that learners did not engage well with formal assessments, it made sense to make the feature less prominent within the UI than I originally imagined.
